The Fractal Escape

From Doom Scrolling to Zoom Scrolling

April 28, 2026  ·  J. Paul Duplantis

Doom scrolling hijacks the primordial brain with a relentless stream of micro-shocks. Zoom scrolling offers a way out — navigating information as a fractal geometry of resonance rather than a flat feed of noise.

It starts with a single thumb-flick. You're waiting for the coffee to brew, or perhaps you're lying in bed, intending to check the weather. Suddenly, the "For You" feed takes the wheel. One minute you're watching a recipe for sourdough; the next, you're four layers deep into a debate about 1990s cinematic tropes, followed by a series of high-octane "fails."

Two hours vanish. Your coffee is cold, your back aches, and your mind feels like it's been scrubbed with steel wool. I am as guilty of this as anyone. There is a specific, haunting realization that hits around the ninety-minute mark — a silent scream in the back of your head saying, "Put the phone down. Escape." But the hand doesn't move. You are locked in a digital tractor beam of nonsense.

The Hijacked Primordial Brain

Why is the escape so difficult? It's because these algorithms aren't talking to your frontal cortex — the seat of your logic, aspirations, and identity. They are whisper-screaming at your primordial brain.

This ancient hardware is designed for survival. It is reactive, not proactive. It prioritizes novelty, threat, and social validation because, in the wild, ignoring a rustle in the grass meant death. Modern feeds exploit this by serving a relentless stream of "micro-shocks." Every scroll is a gamble for a dopamine hit, keeping you in a state of cognitive arrest where your agency is bypassed entirely. You aren't navigating; you are being directed by the noise.

Navigating the Hilbert Space

To break this cycle of linear, algorithmic movement, we have to look toward 3D geometric patterns that map to our own internal signals — the resonance of who we are and who we want to be. This is where the iDIG protocol and the concept of Hilbert Spaces come into play.

In standard computing, information is treated as a flat list — a "silo" of data. But in Quantum Cognition, we can visualize information as vectors within a Hilbert Space. Imagine every piece of data not as a static point on a map, but as a direction in a vast, multi-dimensional geometric realm. Instead of a feed pushing a flat line of content at you, Zoom scrolling allows you to act as an observer navigating a fractal geometry.

Now, I don't use Hilbert Space vectors as rigid, academic math. Some experts might argue I'm coloring outside the lines of "proven" science by applying these concepts to social connection. But then again, science still struggles to explain how two particles can share states across vast distances through entanglement, or why fractal patterns repeat so perfectly throughout the natural world. I believe these aren't just mathematical curiosities; they are hints from nature. I am following those hints to build a language of discovery that feels as organic as the world around us.

A Note on Trust: There is a prerequisite to all of this that cannot be engineered around. In the alignment of vectors, if the observer doesn't trust the connection — the system, the practitioner, or the protocol — resonance is not possible. The signal collapses back into noise before it ever has the chance to align. Trust isn't a feature; it is the foundation on which the entire geometry rests.

The Texture of the Plot: iDIG Movies

The transition to Zoom scrolling is ultimately a shift in who holds the paintbrush. In the current model, the platform paints a mural and forces your eyes to follow the brush. In the iDIG protocol, the observer provides the light, and the system reveals the texture that was already there.

Take my current lab work with iDIG Movies. The protocol isn't just categorizing films by Genre or Director — instead it looks at the fractal texture of a story. This is achieved by parsing the deep narrative prose of plot descriptions from Wikipedia and breaking down the cold data of a "synopsis" into relatable emotional vectors.

Imagine the classic trope of the maverick standing against a corrupt system. A standard algorithm sees this and thinks: "User likes Action or Political Thrillers; show more explosions or courtroom dramas." But by analyzing the linguistic texture of that maverick's journey, we can surface what actually moves you: the specific resonance of principled defiance in the face of overwhelming odds.

In a Hilbert Space, that feeling is a vector. Zoom scrolling allows you to "paint" that preference into the interface by matching the parsed Wikipedia data against the saved resonances in your own observer profile. You aren't navigating to "another movie"; you are zooming into that specific resonance of the lone dissenter. The system surfaces the next "fractal" of that emotional texture — perhaps a scene from a sci-fi epic or a quiet indie drama that shares that exact vector of unyielding integrity — allowing you to explore the depth of a human spirit until you are satisfied, rather than just distracted.

Resolving Human Complexity: Social Worker Alignment

This isn't just for entertainment; it's a tool for high-stakes human empathy. This same "texture-based" navigation is what I'm exploring for Social Worker Alignment.

A social worker today is often buried in a "doom scroll" of fragmented data: police reports, medical records, and housing history. The primordial brain, stressed and overworked, reacts to the "noisy" signal — the most recent crisis. By applying these Hilbert Space vectors, we change how that practitioner "zooms" into a life. Instead of a flat file, they navigate the narrative texture of a human history.

Imagine a first responder at the scene of an overdose involving a former star athlete. To the "reactive" system, this is just another drug-related call — a flat data point of failure. But through the iDIG protocol, we can surface the shared vectors between that athlete's drive and the social worker's mission. We can zoom into the vector of "latent discipline" or the "thirst for peak performance" that has been hijacked by addiction.

When the social worker and the first responder can see those shared geometric patterns, the intervention changes. They aren't just managing a "case"; they are resonating with the underlying strength of the person. They can find the "resonant" intervention — the one small point in the geometry where a specific resource matches a specific human need — without becoming burnt out by the noise of the tragedy.

Bridging the Signal: Zoom Scrolling for the Public Square

While the news module is still on the drawing board, the use case for Zoom scrolling in our civic life is perhaps the most urgent. Currently, we consume political news through a firehose of "breaking" headlines and thirty-second soundbites — isolated, high-friction sparks designed to trigger the primordial brain's alarm system. It is the ultimate doom scroll, leaving us well-informed of the "conflict" but completely disconnected from the "character."

Through the lens of the iDIG protocol, a news item or a political candidate isn't a static headline; they are a collection of vectors. By using LLM enrichment, we can parse the noise of a campaign and surface the signals that bridge the gap between the observer and the person running for office. You aren't just "consuming" an image; you are navigating a path toward resonant understanding — moving from being a passive, exhausted voter to a proactive, engaged observer who can see the human signal through the static of the campaign trail.

Trust

In the context of Hilbert Spaces and Quantum Theory, resonance is not just a calculation; it is a delicate exchange. For two vectors to truly align, there must be a foundation of radical trust. Without it, the signal collapses back into noise.

That is why, alongside the discovery engine, I am building out a Trust Protocol. It is designed to ensure that the Hilbert Space remains a sanctuary. What is consumed or shared remains strictly private by default; the vectors of a person's life are locked away in their own geometric space unless specific, granular permissions are granted. We cannot ask people to navigate deeply if they feel they are being watched by a predatory system. In the iDIG architecture, trust is the "entanglement" that makes the whole system work. We aren't just building for discovery; we are building for a safe, sovereign exchange.

A Call to the Builders

I want to be clear: I am still very much in the lab. This is the experimental stage of the iDIG protocol, where I am moving from theory to "vibe/spec-coding" these architectures. I am building a "Curiosity Engine" that respects the observer's agency, but this is a journey that shouldn't be taken alone.

I am looking to collaborate with other Resonant Builders — engineers, designers, and thinkers who are ready to move past the repetitive loops of the primordial brain. But iDIG is only one experiment around harnessing the geometry of vectors to help people see through the noise. This is less about any one protocol or technology and more about the direction of communication tech.

Maybe this essay is more of a conversation starter than anything else. That when we stop prioritizing "retention" and start building for "resonance," we can collectively help shift society from a state of reactive exhaustion toward one of proactive discovery.

J. Paul Duplantis — Observer, Builder, and founder of i-DIG.io
