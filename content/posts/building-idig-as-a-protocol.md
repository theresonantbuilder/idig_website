There is a version of iDIG that is an application. A tool you open, connect your data to, and use to find signal in the noise of your work. That version is real and I am building it.

But the version that matters most is a **protocol**.

## Why Products Hit Ceilings

Every communication tool that has tried to solve the signal problem has eventually hit the same ceiling: they own the model. The algorithm that decides what is relevant, what aligns, what surfaces — it lives inside their system, trained on their data, optimized for their metrics.

You cannot inspect it. You cannot refine it. You cannot share your definitions of signal with someone else using a different tool.

This is not a technical limitation. It is a design choice. And it is the wrong one.

## What a Protocol Changes

If the signal model is a protocol — a shared specification that any tool can implement — then something different becomes possible.

Your definition of resonance can travel with you. A social worker can define what a high-signal case looks like in their context, and that definition can inform tools they use across their organization. A hiring team can define what meaningful candidate alignment looks like, and that model can be applied consistently across every touchpoint in their process.

The signal becomes yours. Not the platform's.

## The Resonant Builders as the Starting Point

This is why I am building The Resonant Builders alongside iDIG — not as a marketing channel, but as the community where these definitions get stress-tested, debated, and refined.

The protocol will only be as good as the thinking that goes into it. And that thinking needs more than one mind.

If you are working on communication, discovery, or alignment problems in any domain — I would like to hear how you think about signal. That conversation is where the protocol begins.
