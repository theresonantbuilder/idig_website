For most of human history, the bottleneck in communication was transmission. Getting a message from one place to another was the hard part. We solved that. The internet made transmission essentially free and instantaneous.

But we traded one problem for another.

Now the bottleneck is meaning. We are drowning in information and starving for signal. The tools we built to help us communicate — search engines, social feeds, recommendation algorithms — were designed to surface *more*, not to help us understand *better*. They optimized for volume and engagement, not resonance.

This is not a technology failure. It is a design failure. We built pipes when we needed filters. We built amplifiers when we needed tuners.

## The Signal That Gets Lost

Think about the last time you read something that genuinely changed how you saw a problem. That feeling — recognition, resonance, the sense that this idea fits — is not an accident. It is the result of alignment between what the content carries and what you were ready to receive.

Most information tools have no model for that alignment. They match keywords to keywords, clicks to clicks. They have no representation of *meaning* — of what a piece of communication is actually doing, and whether it fits what the reader actually needs.

iDIG is built on a different premise: that meaning behaves like a wave, not a record. It has direction, magnitude, and interference. It can reinforce or cancel. And it can be modeled — not perfectly, but usefully.

## What This Changes

When you treat communication signals as vectors in a shared semantic field, something shifts. You stop asking "what keywords match?" and start asking "what meaning aligns?"

That question opens up possibilities that keyword matching closes off. It surfaces unexpected resonance. It reveals where two things that look different are actually pointing in the same direction — and where two things that look similar are pulling apart.

This is the foundation of iDIG. Not a smarter search engine. A different way of thinking about what communication is, and what tools for communication could be.
