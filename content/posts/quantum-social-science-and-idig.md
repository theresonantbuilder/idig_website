Two years ago I went back into the build. After years of writing essays and recording conversations about the problem of communication and meaning, I decided it was time to test the theories — this time with AI as a collaborator.

What I didn't expect was to find the mathematical language I had been missing for twenty years inside a field I had never heard of: **Quantum Social Science**.

## What Quantum Social Science Actually Says

Quantum Social Science is not a metaphor. It is a research program — developed by scholars including Jerome Busemeyer and Peter Bruza — that applies the mathematical formalism of quantum mechanics to the structure of human cognition and decision-making.

The core insight is this: human judgment does not behave like classical probability. People's responses to questions change based on the order in which questions are asked. Context shapes meaning in ways that cannot be captured by fixed probability distributions.

Quantum probability handles this naturally. And the mathematical space it operates in — **Hilbert space** — turns out to be exactly the right structure for representing meaning.

## Hilbert Space Vectors and Communication

In Hilbert space, a vector is not just a direction. It is a state of possibility — a superposition of potential meanings that collapses into something specific when observed in context.

When I mapped this onto communication signals, everything clicked.

A piece of text is not a bag of keywords. It is a vector — with direction (what it is pointing toward), magnitude (how strongly it carries that meaning), and interference (how it interacts with other signals in the same semantic space).

Two signals that look similar on the surface may be pointing in opposite directions. Two signals that use different vocabulary may be pointing at exactly the same thing.

iDIG is built to surface that geometry.
