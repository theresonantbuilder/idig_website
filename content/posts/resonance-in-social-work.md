Of all the domains where the iDIG framework could make a meaningful difference, social work is the one that keeps pulling me back.

The challenge is not a lack of resources. In most communities, the resources exist — social workers, first responders, community organizations, mental health support. The challenge is **alignment**. Getting the right support to the right person at the right moment, when the window to help is often very narrow.

Current systems were not built for this. Case management software tracks what happened. It does not surface what is about to happen, or who is best positioned to respond.

## What Signal-Driven Alignment Would Change

Imagine a system that could surface — in real time — which cases are showing escalating signals across multiple touchpoints. Not based on a single data point, but on the interference pattern of communication across calls, visits, reports, and prior contacts.

That is not surveillance. It is resonance. It is the difference between a system that flags keywords and a system that understands what those keywords mean in context.

A social worker who can see that a family's signals are pointing in a particular direction — not just what was reported, but what the pattern implies — can make a fundamentally different kind of decision about how and when to intervene.

## Why This Matters for the Protocol

This use case is why iDIG needs to be a protocol rather than a proprietary product. The signal definitions that matter in social work are different from those in hiring or commerce. But the underlying structure — vectors in a shared semantic field, visible and inspectable by the people responsible for outcomes — is the same.

The protocol makes it possible to build domain-specific signal models on a shared foundation. That is the architecture that serves both a social worker and a talent team and a reader looking for their next book — without collapsing their different definitions of resonance into a single algorithm none of them control.
